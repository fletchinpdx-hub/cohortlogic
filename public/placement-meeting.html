How to run a class placement meeting

The lists aren’t the hard part. Getting your team to agree on them is.

Cohort Logic team · July 2026 · 6 min read

This guide assumes you’re coming in with draft lists already built. If you’re not there yet: What “balanced classes” actually means, and how to measure it →

Most schools treat the placement meeting as the place where lists get finalized. In practice, it’s often the place where lists get relitigated, where one teacher’s concerns dominate the room, and where you leave with changes you’re not sure about.

It doesn’t have to go that way. The meeting is valuable: teachers know things about students that won’t show up in any data file. The goal is to capture that knowledge without letting the meeting become a negotiation where whoever speaks loudest wins.

Who should be in the room

The meeting works best with the current-year teachers for the grade being placed, the receiving teachers for next year if possible, a counselor if your school has one, and an administrator who can make final calls.

Keep the group small enough that everyone can speak. Twelve people around a table trying to review 300 student placements is not a meeting, it’s a performance.

A few people who should not be in the room: parents, outside advocates, and anyone who has a personal stake in a specific placement outcome. That’s not cynicism. It’s a recognition that the meeting can only work if people can speak candidly about students, and they won’t if the room includes someone who will react to what they hear.

What to bring

Come in with draft lists that already satisfy your constraints. If you’re walking into the meeting with known violations, address them before you go in. The meeting should be a review, not a first pass.

Print or project the lists by class, not by teacher name if assignments aren’t final. You want people reacting to the composition of the class, not to who’s teaching it.

Have your balance metrics ready. Means by class, flag counts by class. If someone claims a class is unbalanced, you want to be able to check that claim against actual numbers rather than impressions.

Bring a clear constraint list. If a teacher asks why two students are in the same class, you should be able to show that no separation request existed for them, or explain why a different constraint took priority.

How to structure the time

The opening five minutes matter more than most facilitators realize. Set the purpose clearly: you’re here to surface things the data can’t capture. Academic history, behavioral patterns, sibling dynamics, peer relationships that should be considered. You are not here to reassign students based on teacher preference or workload concerns.

Go grade by grade. For each grade, give a brief overview of the balance metrics, then open it up for specific student-level knowledge. Move through grades on a fixed timeline and hold it.

Take every concern seriously and write it down. Not every concern will change a placement, but teachers need to feel heard, especially for the ones that don’t.

The request problem

At some point, a teacher will ask to move a specific student into or out of their class. Sometimes the reason is legitimate: genuine knowledge about a peer relationship or a dynamic that isn’t captured anywhere in writing. Sometimes the reason is that the teacher wants fewer students with intensive needs, or wants a particular student because they know the family.

The only requests worth acting on are those grounded in specific, articulable student-level information that you didn’t already have. "I know these two had a falling out over the summer" is worth hearing. "I’d prefer a lighter load" is not a placement reason.

Set this expectation at the start of the meeting, not after the first problematic request comes in.

Making decisions that hold

Every change made in the meeting should be evaluated against the balance metrics before it’s accepted. Moving one student can shift the IEP count in a class, affect the academic mean, or break a constraint you already satisfied.

The administrator in the room needs to be willing to say no. Not to every request, but to the ones that don’t hold up. If every concern results in a change, you’ll leave with lists that no longer reflect the criteria you set when you built them.

Close the meeting with a clear statement: the lists are final as of this date. Changes after this point require sign-off from the principal and must be documented. This is not about being rigid. It is about protecting the integrity of a process that took real work.

After the meeting

Make any agreed changes, verify the balance metrics still clear your thresholds, and re-check constraints. Then send the finalized lists to whoever needs them and document what changed from the draft and why.

If a parent calls to dispute a placement, you want to be able to explain the process, show that it was consistent, and point to the criteria that were applied. That’s only possible if you kept a record of what the meeting produced and how you got there.

The short version
  • ✓Come in with balanced draft lists, not a first pass. The meeting is a review, not the build.
  • ✓Keep the room small. No parents, no advocates, no one with a stake in a specific outcome.
  • ✓Only act on requests grounded in specific student-level information you didn’t already have.
  • ✓Check balance metrics after every change. One move can shift more than you expect.
  • ✓Close with a clear finalization date and a documented record of what changed.
